Abyssal Zone

The
Abyssal Plains

Covering over 60% of Earth's surface, the abyssal plains are the largest ecosystem on the planet — yet the least explored. Snow-like particles called "marine snow" drift down constantly, carrying carbon from the surface to the deep — making the ocean a critical climate regulator.

What lives here? +
Sea cucumbers dominate the abyssal plains, making up 90% of the biomass in some areas. They process sediment like earthworms, recycling nutrients essential to the entire ecosystem.
How deep is the ocean floor? +
The average depth of the ocean floor is around 3,688 metres — but the abyssal plains sit at 4,000–6,000 metres in vast, flat expanses formed by the accumulation of sediment over millions of years.
Marine snow — what is it? +
Marine snow is a shower of organic material falling from upper ocean zones. It includes dead organisms, faecal matter, and mucus — yet it is the primary food source for abyssal life and sequesters vast amounts of carbon.
Hydrothermal vents +
Discovered in 1977, hydrothermal vents are geothermal cracks on the ocean floor. Superheated water rich in minerals gushes out, supporting entire ecosystems that live without sunlight — powered by chemosynthesis.

The Hadal Zone

Deeper than Mount Everest is tall. The Mariana Trench descends to nearly 11,000 metres — a world of perpetual night, near-freezing temperatures, and pressure 1,100 times greater than at sea level.

Yet even here, life endures. Hadal snailfish, crustaceans, and single-celled organisms thrive in conditions we once thought impossible. The ocean's deepest secret is that it is never truly empty.
